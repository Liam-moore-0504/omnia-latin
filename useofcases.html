Use of Cases

NominativeExample
The nominative case usually denotes the subject of a verbPhilosophia animum fōrmat
Philosophy shapes the mind

Occidit brevis lūx
The brief light dies
The nominative can also denote the complementdeus rēctor est
god is the ruler
-
GenetiveExample
The genitive case typically denotes the possessor of somethingŌrīonis ēnsem
Orion's sword

Ex urbe hostium
From the cityof the enemy
Genitive of description (= genitive of quality) – denotes a particular quality of somethingarmillās magnī ponderis
braceletes of great weight
Genitive of quantity (= partitive genitive) – denotes the whole, of which something is a part Nōn multum malī
Not muchevil

prōvinciālium validissimīs
the strongest of the provincial inhabitants
Genitive of value – denotes the value of something, particularly after verbs of estimating and valuingomnēs ūnius aestimēmus assis
let us value them all at a single penny

aliās haud magnī facere
he was not much concerned about the others
Objective genitive – denotes what would be the object, if the phrase were expressed by a verb (he desires the sky, they compete for honor)caelī cupīdine
by desire for the sky

honōris aemulātiō
competition for honor
With words of remembering and forgettingnec mē meminisse pigēbit Elissae
nor will it displease me to remember Elissa

nostrī memorem lūctūs
commemorative of our grief
With other verbs and adjectiveshominēs bellandī cupidī
men desirous of making war

frūgis egentēs
lacking grain
DativeExample
The dative case typically denotes the indirect object, i.e. beneficiary or recipient, of an action, and is often translated to or forDedit ōscula nātō
He gave kisses to his son

Convehunt omnia gulae
they gather everything for their appetite
Dative of agent – denotes the agent of a passive verb (instead of ā/ab + ablative) or a periphrastic passive verbOmnibus ille diēs nōbīs nātālis agātur
let that birthday be celebrated by all of us

ego, quī nōn intellegor ūllī
I, who am not understood by anyone

ubi diūtius nōbīs habitandum est
where we must live for a longer time
Dative of possession – denotes the person to whom something belongs Quicquid erit, erit tibi
whatever there will be, will be yours

Cui digitī pinguēs erit
Whose fingers will be fat
Dative of purpose – denotes the purpose of an actionOmnibusTerra dīvitiīs foditur
the earth is dug into for wealth

Collēctam exsiliō pūbem
young people assembled for exile

ūniversōs cūrae habuit
He treated them all considerately
Dative of reference – often denotes the person with whom an opinion is associated omnia mihi tempora sunt misera
for me all the times are wretched
Dative of result – denotes the result of a situation negōtium laetitiae fuit potius quam timōrī
the affair was a reason for rejoicing rather than fear
Dative of separation – particularly with reference to people mihi nūmen ēripuit mentem
a divine power snatched my mind from me

nē sit pāx adēmpta mihi
lest peace be taken away from me
Double dative – combining a dative of purpose or result with a dative of reference erit mihi cūrae
it will be for a concern to me (= I will take care)

quid aliud exitiō Lacedaemoniīs et Athēniēnsibus fuit?
what other thing caused the destruction of the Spartans and Athenians?
With compound verbs and some other verbs, including crēdō, imperō, suādeō omnibus incutiēns amōrem
instilling love in all

tēctīs succēdite nostrīs
enter our houses
AccusativeExample
The accusative case denotes the direct object of a verb ōtium et rēgēs et beātās urbēs perdidit
leisure has destroyed both kings and prosperous cities

philosophia animum fōrmat
philosophy shapes the mind
Accusative of respect – specifies that to which an adjective or participle applies nūdus membra
naked in regard to his limbs (= bare-limbed)
Accusative of time – denotes how long something lasts apud Flaccum diēs xiii fuimus
I have been at Flaccus’ house for thirteen days
In indirect statements, the accusative is used to denote the subject of the infinitive rūmōrēs diffūdērunt gentēs arctōās cāsūs versāre
rumors spread that the peoples in the north were stirring up troubles
With certain prepositions – including ad, apud, in, per, post, prope, trāns trāns Rhēnum
across the Rhine

celerēs per aurās
through the swift breezes
AblativeExample
The ablative case typically denotes location, relative position, and the means or instrument by which an action is achieved. It is often represented in English using in, from, with, or by.
Ablative absolute – providing context or information grammatically separate from the rest of the sentence salūte datā in vicem redditāque
once a greeting had been given and received

palam, spectantibus omnibus
openly, while everyone was watching
Ablative of agent – denotes the agent of a passive verb (usually with ā/ab) multī ā mē virī praedicārentur
many men might be named by me

iussus est ab Hilariānō prōicī
he was ordered by Hilarian to be thrown out
Ablative of cause – denotes the cause of an action similitūdine vītae et spē eiusdem licentiae
because of the similarity of their way of life and their hope of the same license

id hōc facilius eīs persuāsit
he persuaded them more easily because of this
Ablative of comparison – as an alternative to *quam* dulcius urbe quid est?
what is more pleasant than the city?
Ablative of degree of difference – with comparatives paulō plēniōre
a little more full

multō inīquiōre
much more unjust
Ablative of description – describes a quality or characteristic fuisse trāditur colōre candidō
he is said to have been of fair complexion

parilī aetāte Philēmōn
Philemon, equally old
Ablative of instrument – denotes the means with which something is achieved teguntur lūmina nocte
my eyes are covered by darkness

ōraque caeruleā aquā excipiuntur
his mouth is received by the blue-gray water
Ablative of place – denotes where something happens (usually with a preposition) fornācibus ignis anhēlat
in the furnaces the fire emits a hot blast

illā casā cōnsenuēre
they had grown old in that cottage
Ablative of price – denotes the specific cost of an item cum quīnque sēstertiīs ēmisset piscem
since he had bought the fish for five thousand sesterces
Ablative of separation – denotes separation (often with a preposition) quae numquam potest dēmovērī locō
which can never be moved from its place

ut dēmātur ōtiō nausea
so that tedium may be removed from leisure
Ablative of specification – specifies a quality or state (also called ablative of respect) animīs opibusque parātī
ready in their spirits and resources

Helvētiī reliquōs Gallōs virtūte praecēdunt
the Helvetii surpass the remaining Gauls in virtue
Ablative of time – denotes when or within which something happens Quīnctius sextō decimō diē sē abdicāvit
Cincinnatus resigned on the sixteenth day

innumerābilia accidunt singulīs hōrīs
countless things happen every hour
With certain prepositions – including ā/ab, cum, ē/ex, in, prō, sine ex nostrā prōvinciā
from our province

prō aureīs dōnīs
instead of golden gifts
With certain verbs – including potior, ūtor, fruor, fungor, vēscor vēscimur bēstiīs
we feed on wild beasts

dentibus atque comīs ūteris ēmptīs
you use bought teeth and hair
VocativeExample
The vocative case is used for direct address Attice, crēde mihi
Atticus, believe me

ō fōns Bandusiae
o spring of Bandusia
LocativeExample
The locative case denotes the place where something happens ut Alexandrīae contigit
as happened at Alexandria

domī nihil erat
there was nothing at home
